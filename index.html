The IPO Learning Method
Input → Practice → Output · For every subject · Every session
Every study session follows 3 steps: SHORT input (video/reading, max 30 min), HEAVY practice (problems, no shortcuts), and OUTPUT (explain it in your own words). Passive watching = not studying. Output is the proof of learning.
📐 MATH
INPUT
1 Khan Academy video (max 15 min). Watch with paper — write every formula they use. Don't just watch.
PRACTICE
15–20 problems minimum, paper + pen, no calculator for basics. Stuck? Try 5 more minutes before looking at solutions. Mark every wrong answer.
OUTPUT
Say the rule out loud: "The chain rule says..." — if you can't explain it without notes in 60 seconds, you don't know it yet. Error log: re-solve every wrong answer.
🧬 BIOLOGY
INPUT
CrashCourse or NCBI diagram (max 20 min). Draw the process as you watch — cells, cycles, pathways. Visual encoding sticks longer.
PRACTICE
Draw from memory: close your notes, draw the cell / process / cycle. Label every part. Check against your notes. Repeat until 100% correct.
OUTPUT
"Feynman it": Explain what you just learned to someone (or to yourself) as if they're 12 years old. If you stumble, go back and re-read that part only.
⚗️ CHEMISTRY
INPUT
CrashCourse Chemistry (max 15 min) + write the key reaction/formula. Don't skip writing — your hand remembers what your eyes forget.
PRACTICE
Balance 8–10 equations. Start with simple ones, progress to harder. For stoichiometry: always show units, always check molar mass. Wrong units = wrong answer.
OUTPUT
Write the "why": don't just solve, explain why the equation is balanced or why a bond forms. Lab reports later will thank you for this habit.
⚡ PHYSICS
INPUT
CrashCourse Physics (max 15 min). Sketch the free-body diagram or velocity graph as you watch. Physics lives in diagrams, not just formulas.
PRACTICE
10 word problems — draw a diagram first for every single one, before touching a formula. List knowns and unknowns explicitly. This habit will save you in exams.
OUTPUT
Real-world connection: for each topic, name one thing in daily life that demonstrates it. "Newton's 3rd Law is why a basketball bounces." Anchoring = long-term memory.

Minimum Viable Daily System
For low-motivation days · Never skip completely · Always do something
The Rule: On bad days, you don't cancel — you downgrade. This is your emergency mode. 3 non-negotiables that take 30–45 minutes total. No skipping. Zero. Even if you're sick, tired, or lazy — you still do these 3.
01
Re-read your error log (10 min)
Open your notebook. Read your past wrong answers. Just read — no solving required. You're reminding your brain of patterns to avoid. This alone is more useful than a new video.
02
Solve 5 easy problems (15 min)
Pick the subject you feel least bad about today. Solve 5 problems you already understand. This is momentum maintenance, not new learning. Done is better than perfect.
03
Write 3 sentences about what you know (10 min)
Pick any concept from this week. Write 3 sentences explaining it. That's it. Writing forces recall. It's the simplest and most underrated study technique.
If you finish these 3 and feel better → continue normally. If you still feel awful → stop. Streaks don't survive burnout. Streaks survive sustainable systems. Rest is not failure. Skipping everything is failure.

Communication Improvement System
Daily writing structure · Daily speaking drills · 15 min/day total
✍️ WRITING — WHAT · HOW · WHY STRUCTURE
Every day, write 1 paragraph using this exact 3-part structure. Takes 5–7 minutes. After 2 weeks it becomes automatic — and it's the same structure used in lab reports and thesis writing.
📝 Daily Writing Drill (5–7 min)
Every day
WHAT — State the concept in 1 sentence. "The mitochondria is the energy-producing organelle of the cell."
HOW — Explain the mechanism in 1–2 sentences. "It converts glucose to ATP through a process called cellular respiration, using oxygen."
WHY — State why it matters. "Without this process, cells would have no energy to perform any function — from muscle movement to DNA replication."

🗣️ SPEAKING — 1-MINUTE EXPLANATION DRILLS
🎙 Daily Speaking Drill (5–8 min)
Night — after peak study
Step 1 — Pick the hardest thing you studied today. Set a 60-second timer on your phone.
Step 2 — Explain it out loud — to nobody, to the wall, or to a family member. No notes. No reading.
Step 3 — Record yourself (voice memo on your phone). Listen back once. Notice filler words: "um", "uh", "kumbaga", "like".
Step 4 — Try again. Replace every filler word with a silent pause. A confident pause sounds like a choice.
After 30 days: You'll notice you can explain any concept in 60 seconds without stumbling. This is exactly what your professors will ask in oral recitations at Mapúa. You'll be prepared while others panic.

Musicology is your long-game investment in neuroscience curiosity. Music is deeply mathematical (rhythm, frequency ratios), physically complex (acoustics, wave mechanics), and neurologically rich (perception, memory encoding). It's not wasted time — it's interdisciplinary thinking. But it has a strict time budget.
🎛 TIME BUDGET — NON-NEGOTIABLE
Total weekly study time: ~35 hours → Max 3.5 hours for Side Quest (10%)
Realistic target: 30–60 min on Saturday only. Not daily. Not during peak hours.
7% of weekly time · Safe zone ✓
🧠 HOW TO CONNECT MUSICOLOGY TO YOUR MAIN GOAL
MathMusic theory IS math. Time signatures are fractions. Intervals are frequency ratios. Chords are harmonic series. Study equal temperament — it's a logarithmic scale identical to decibels in physics.
PhysicsSound is a mechanical wave. Study how frequency (Hz), amplitude, and wavelength create what you hear. This directly connects to wave mechanics in Physics 1.
NeuroMusic engages more brain regions simultaneously than any other human activity. Study the neuroscience of music perception — this is directly relevant to a biological engineering + neuroscience long-term path.

Anti-Procrastination Rules
No tomorrow rule · No shiny objects · No passive learning
These are not suggestions. They are rules. Every rule has a reason. Every rule has been tested by students who got overwhelmed. They exist to protect you from yourself — especially in the early weeks before discipline builds.
🚫 ANTI-PROCRASTINATION
⏰
The "No Tomorrow" Rule
If something is scheduled for today, it happens today. "I'll do it tomorrow" is the most expensive lie students tell themselves. Tomorrow-you is not smarter, more motivated, or more capable than today-you. They're the same person with less time.
🕐
The 2-Minute Start Rule
When you don't want to study, open your notebook and write the date. That's your only commitment. The act of starting almost always creates momentum. Set a 2-minute timer. At 2 minutes, decide: continue or stop. You will almost always continue.
📵
The Phone Distance Rule
Your phone goes in another room during peak hours — not silent, not face-down, not in your pocket. Another room. Every notification is a context switch that costs 15–20 minutes of refocus time. During peak hours, you don't have that time to waste.
✨ ANTI-SHINY OBJECT SYNDROME
📌
One System Rule
This planner is your system. You don't need another app, another study method, another YouTube productivity channel. Every hour you spend planning is an hour you didn't spend learning. Curiosity about new methods is procrastination in disguise.
🗂️
The "Parking Lot" for Ideas
When a new interesting topic distracts you mid-study ("ooh, what is CRISPR?", "I should learn Python"), write it in a physical parking lot list. That list gets reviewed on Sunday only. Your focus block is sacred — ideas get parked, not chased.
📺 ANTI-PASSIVE LEARNING
✏️
Write While You Watch Rule
You never watch a study video without a pen in your hand. Write every formula, every diagram, every key term as it appears. If you can't write it, pause the video. Watching without writing is entertainment, not studying.
🔒
No Video Without Problems After Rule
You never finish a video without immediately solving at least 5 related problems. Videos create the illusion of understanding. Problems expose what you actually know. Comfort with explanations ≠ ability to solve. Test yourself or the video was wasted.
📓
The Error Log Is Not Optional
Every wrong answer gets written in your physical error log with: (1) what you got wrong, (2) why you got it wrong, (3) the correct approach. Your error log is your personalized exam study guide. Students who use error logs outperform those who don't — every time.
The Only Metric That Matters
You are not measured by how many videos you watched, how many notes you highlighted, or how long you sat at your desk. You are measured by how many problems you solved correctly that you couldn't solve last week. That's it. That's the whole game.
